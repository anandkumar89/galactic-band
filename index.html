⚠️ Correction — Galactic Core vs. Milky Way Band
The original itinerary says "Milky Way from 9:30 PM onwards" — partially correct: the faint galactic band is visible overhead all night. But the galactic core (bright colourful bulge — what everyone wants to photograph) rises at ~1:27–1:31 AM IST (geometric horizon) and reaches a usable altitude of 10° by ~2:27–2:31 AM. It peaks at ~29° maximum — about three fist-widths above the southern horizon. This gives a solid 2.5–3 hour shooting window before dawn. The core is an evening object from May–June onward.

How to Spot the Milky Way

🌌 Field Guide to Finding the Galaxy

STEP 01 — ADAPT

Give your eyes 20–30 minutes to dark-adapt. Use a red headlamp only — white light resets rod cells instantly.

STEP 02 — ORIENT

In March pre-dawn, face south-southeast. The galactic core rises near Sagittarius and Scorpius. It clears the horizon around 1:20–1:30 AM IST and reaches a usable height (~10°) by ~2:20–2:30 AM. Maximum altitude is ~29° — about three fist-widths above the horizon.

STEP 03 — AVERTED VISION

Don't stare directly — look slightly to the side. In Spiti's Bortle Class 1 sky, the Milky Way casts a faint visible shadow on the ground.

STEP 04 — USE APPS

Download Stellarium, PhotoPills, or SkySafari offline before leaving Shimla. They show exact core rise/set times and allow AR pre-framing in the field.

STEP 05 — FIND FOREGROUND

Scout in daylight. Best spots: Dhankar Lake viewpoint, open fields behind Tara House in Mudh, Kinner Kailash ridge from Kalpa.

STEP 06 — COLD PREP

Nights drop to -10°C to -20°C. Keep batteries inside your jacket. Bring a thermos. Set a timer — hypothermia sneaks up when you're stationary.

Photography Techniques
📷 Camera Settings
  • ISO: 1600–6400 (start at 3200, adjust for noise)
  • Aperture: f/1.4–f/2.8 — as wide as your lens allows
  • Shutter: 500 rule — 500 ÷ focal length = max seconds. 24mm → ~20 sec before star trails
  • Focus: Manual, live view at 10× on a bright star. AF hunts and fails
  • White Balance: 3500–4200K for natural dark-blue sky; refine in RAW
  • Format: Always RAW — dynamic range is essential for post-processing
  • Intervalometer: For timelapse or avoiding shake on bulb exposures
🎯 Composition & Technique
  • Rule of Thirds: Band along diagonal third; monastery or peak in lower third
  • Stacking: 10–20 identical frames stacked in Sequator (free) — dramatic noise reduction
  • Star Trails: f/8, ISO 400, hundreds of 30-sec frames merged in StarStax
  • Light painting: Torch or phone to illuminate monastery walls during long exposure
  • Panorama: 5–8 overlapping vertical frames; stitch in Lightroom/PTGui for 180° arch
  • Gear: Wide-angle 14–24mm; sturdy tripod; remote shutter; 3× spare batteries
✏️ Post-Processing (RAW)
  • Noise: Luminance NR 40–70, Color NR 40–60 in Lightroom; or Topaz DeNoise AI
  • Clarity: Dehaze +20–40, Clarity +10–20 to pull out nebula texture
  • Colour grade: Highlights toward teal/blue, shadows toward magenta-purple
  • Stretch histogram: Lift exposure +1–2 EV, then pull highlights back
  • Sky mask: Lightroom AI masking — process sky and foreground independently
  • Sharpen stars: Use a luminosity mask only — avoids amplifying noise in darks
⚡ Spiti-Specific Tips
  • Altitude: Don't do a full late-night shoot on your first Spiti night — AMS peaks overnight
  • Lens fogging: Wrap a hand warmer in a sock around the lens barrel
  • Sand drift: High-altitude desert — keep lens caps on between exposures
  • Power banks: 20,000 mAh+ fully charged in Shimla; homestay charging is unreliable
  • No ATMs beyond Recong Peo — carry cash. No data above Nako most nights
  • Prime spots: Dhankar Lake, Mudh open fields, Kinner Kailash ridge from Kalpa
